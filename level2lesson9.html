    • The Past

    The creation of the past tense in Ceron is quite simple compared to some other tenses. In Ceron, there is only one true way to create the past tense, (although different particles can be added on to give a different nuance). The way we create the past tense in Ceron is by attaching this to the end of a verb.

    Ara / Ra

    Generally speaking, “ara” gives the impression that the past you are describing was not too long ago, similar to in English where you can add “just” in front of a verb. This past tense form can only be used with animate things, meaning, you cannot use “ara” in a sentence like “The house was big” but you can use it in a sentence like “He was big”. The process to use “ara” with inanimate objects is slightly more complex, and so I will explain it in a separate paragraph. Nevertheless, here are some past-tense sentences in Ceron.

    Ylahabera manaraya

    I did not buy this book

    Anthanasa runsona aurorenaryara

    She approached him in a loud room

    Yrasmarasona bodara

    They ate fish

    Ymamona sara

    I went home

    Marason marasasa abodara

    The fish ate the river

    Pretty simple right? Now, whenever you want to express the past tense in Ceron with inanimate objects, the process becomes more confusing. In order to create the past tense, you must use this form.

    Dac(d) - V/A - (N)

    Dont panic! This sequence is actually just a combination of smaller grammatical particles which you haven't learned yet, but for the sake of understanding, I’ll break it down quickly. The first word, “dacd” (dac when the following verb begins with a consonant) literally means “remember” or “remembered” or “said”. The “V” represents the verb which you are trying to put into the past tense, and the “N” represents the normalization marker, which is used to turn verbs into adjectives which can then modify the noun that follows. (Which is the inanimate object). For example,

    Ca dacdundara lu

    Here flowers bloomed

    Here, “dacdundar(a)” means “remembered-bloom” which means “bloomed”, always remember that there is no space between dac(d) and the verb. Regarding the normalization particle, NP verbs take “a”, whereas P verbs take “u”. This concept of the verb coming before the object might seem confusing since not too long ago I stated that the verb always comes last in a sentence. Here though, the verb isn't actually acting as a verb, and so can be placed before the object. Here are some more examples, (Also, in the case of adjectives, they are treated as if they were a verb, just without the nominalizing particle attached, and instead just the particle indicating whether or not they are a P or NP adjective.)

    Dacsa mamon

    The house was big

    Dacdagada maha

    The water was red

    The next tense is... the present tense!

    The Present

    In Ceron, all verbs are originally in their present tense form, and so nothing must be done in order to indicate the present tense.

    The Future

    In Ceron, the future is created quite simply, all you need to do is add “ra” before the verb you want to turn into the future tense

    R / Ra - V

    Now generally speaking, “ra” is used to create the standard future tense in Ceron, as it’s rather simple to understand, and almost directly corresponds with the English future tense. The origin of “ra” is unclear, and unfortunately, congjuating “ra” with verbs is extremely, extremely irregular, to the point where almost every common-verb you use will probably have an irregular future-tense conjugation.

    Ylahabera ramana

    I will not buy this book

    Anthanasa arabuden

    She will speak to him

    Yrasmarasona rabod

    They will eat fish

    Ymamona rasa

    I will go home

    Marason marasasa araboda

    The fish will not eat the river

    Of course, there are so many other ways to express all of these tenses, but since this is just the 9th lesson, I won't delve deep into all the different forms just yet.

    Expressing More, Alot, All and Some

    In Ceron, expressing “more” of something is relatively simple. All you need to do is add “mana” after the noun you wish to modify. However, in English, we can also say things like “I run more” or “I talk more”, where we are expressing doing “more” of a verb, instead of a noun. In Ceron, this is not possible, or rather, it takes more steps to express “more” with verbs. In order to say a sentence like “I talk more”, you have to turn the verb “talk”, into a noun by attaching -u to the end of it (this is nominalization) and then adding “mana” after the verb (which is now a noun). I also want to preface that, in Ceron, we don't differentiate between “more” and “alot”. So the sentence “I talk more”, can also mean “I talk alot”. The concept of “more…than” is also a completely different grammatical form in Ceron, so please don't confuse them. Anyways, here are some examples,

    Yabudenu mana

    I talk alot / I talk more

    Ythanasya arenu manara

    I visited him alot / I visited him more

    Ancarnac mana

    She has alot of pride

    Ydehade manaya

    I dont have alot of strength

    Ymaha ramana

    I will have alot of water

    Translating “all” into Ceron is not as simple as it may seem. In Ceron, “all” is represented in the form of “-c” or “-ac” at the end of most nouns. For example,

    Maru erac

    All the sounds of the ocean

    Anconac

    All the students

    But when a verb is introduced into the sentence, “sa” or “asa” must be placed before the verb (attached to the end of the noun before without a space), (of course, alongside “-c” or “-ac”)

    Anconac concensa assomer

    All the students have uniforms

    Denac madasa amuna*

    All the people are saving money

    In Ceron, once again, we use different versions of the word “some” depending on whether or not the word following it is inanimate or animate. For inanimate things, the word “Maru”, which is actually a derivation of “Marep”, and the possessive particle “-U”, is used, and is placed before said word (inanimate noun). Here are some examples,

    Maru concen ya

    Some uniforms are white

    Maru munur thada

    Some doors are blue

    Ymaru maha abod

    I am drinking some water

    With animate objects, “Maru” turns into “Marud”, which is from “Maru” and “Dena” (Person). Once again, “Marud” is placed before the animate noun it is modifying.

    Marud dena abudena

    Some people are not talking

    Marud dancona cenu sharona

    Some male students like to fool around

    Regarding verbs, in order to express that you do a verb “sometimes” or, “some”, all you need to do is nominalize the verb by attaching -u to the end of it, and then attaching -mar directly onto the end of the now nominalized verb. (Without a space). You’ll notice that whenever more than one verb appears in a sentence in Ceron, the verb in front of the other verb is always nominalized. For example, in the last sentence above, the verb “cen” meaning “to fool around” is turned into “cenu” which means “fooling around” (noun), and then is followed by “sharon”. Here are some examples of “some” with verbs,

    Ymaha abodumar

    I drink water sometimes NOT I drink some water

    Ymona amanu sharonamarara

    I like to buy sun hats sometimes

    Thanaunurumar

    He sometimes works out

    *When there is more than one verbs in a singular clause, the verb in which -mar is attached to is not nominalized, but instead left in its regular form.
