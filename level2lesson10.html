    • Asking Questions

    Asking questions in Ceron is quite simple compared to English. In English, not only do we raise our intonation when asking questions, we also use a plethora of different question words, and switch up the word order, just to ask a question. In Ceron, none of this is necessary. Instead, we just use this particle one basic particle

    Anya

    This question particle can be attached to nouns, verbs, adjectives etc. When using this particle, you never need to raise your intonation or change the word order. Earlier, you might have seen the use of “nya” and “ya” used to create questions. The question particle “anya” (shown above), is actually only used by a woman or man who is talking to someone when they don't know their gender. Usually, we use these more gendered question particles when speaking to people

    Nya

    (Man to Woman)

    Ya

    (Man to Man)

    Nya

    (Woman to Man)

    Nnya

    (Woman to Woman)

    You then can place these particles at the end of the sentence that you wish to turn into a question

    Andun mamon a sa sya nya?

    What time are you coming home? (From woman to man)

    Au ana sa aysa ya

    Do you love her? (From man to man)

    þan maha a byod a nya?

    Is he drinking water? (From man to woman)

    Au gala somer nnya?

    Do you have a dog? (From woman to woman)

    Au latanardun somera nya?

    Do you not have a train ticket? (From woman to man)

    Nyabu a buden a nnya?

    Do you speak Ceron? (From woman to woman)

    Once again, by including “a” before the question particle, it creates a sense of respect and formality, it does not represent the verb “to be” in this instance.

    Now other than just adding a question particle to the end of a sentence, you can also use “question words” such as what,where,when etc. Almost all Ceronec question words come from the same core word

    An

    What

    Although directly translated this word means “what”, it's actually never used on its own. Instead, a more specific type of “what” is used in its stead.

    Andun

    When

    Ana

    Where

    Anden

    Who/Why

    Now, you might be wondering, none of these words translate to “what”, so how do you express “what” if “an” is not used? In Ceron, it is extremely extremely uncommon to ask someone a question like “What did you eat?” or “What did you buy?” Instead, people just ask “You ate?” or “You bought?”. For example, in Lesson 9, there is a question “Andun nya?” which literally means “When?” but is translated into English as “What did you do?”. Now, when you want to know specifically “what” someone is doing or “what” someone bought, you can use “an” although it is always connected to the end of the verb in a sentence

    Andun sya nya?

    When did you come?

    Andena sa au a buden ya?

    Who are you talking to?

    Manan nya?

    What did you buy?

    Ana haberon a nnya?

    Where is the library?

    These question words are tenseless, and so can represent the future, past or present. If you want to emphasis that the question you are asking is in the present tense, you can add “a” after the question word

    Ana a au a sa

    Where are you going (emphasis on now)

    Manana nya?

    What are you buying (emphasis on currently)

    There are also two different types of “how” question words in Ceron

    Sona

    How Many

    Mananch

    How much

    Expressing “how” in Ceron is a little more complicated to express compared to English, and so I will save it for later. Here are some examples. “Sona” can only be attached to the ends of nouns, “Mananch” on the other hand, can only appear as the first word in a sentence and is not connected to a word.

    Eras galasona a somer anya?

    How many dogs are they carrying

    Mananch mad somer nnya?

    How much money do you have?

    Au masona a sa a buden ya?

    How many friends are you talking to?

    Expressing “With”

    Now in Ceron there are many different ways to express “with”, which change depending on the type of words you are connecting together!

    U

    With (with non-living nouns)

    Sa ra

    With (with two living nouns)

    Sana

    With (with one living and one non-living noun)

    Let's break them down one by one. The first one, “u”, is used with two non-living nouns. That means you can use “u” in sentences like "love with pain" and "water with lemon". We also use “u” to describe two colors together, so the sentence “This book is red and blue” is “This book is red with blue” in Ceron. This “preposition” is attached directly onto the end of the first word. Here are some examples of that in sentences

    E lemonu cucumbya maha shallona ra

    I'm pretty sure I liked water with lemons and cucumbers

    Lahaber tagadu ogad a

    This book is red and green

    Now this next type of “with” is probably the most common one since it's used with two living-nouns. This means you can use it in sentences like “I saw you with her” or “I am with my boyfriend” etc. We create sentences like these using “sa ra”. “Sa ra” is always attached to the end of a word, and it is not necessary to include the verb “to be” in sentences with it

    E ronalla þanmasa ra a buden

    I talking with my boyfriend at the movie theatre (More naturally in English: Im talking to my boyfriend at the movie theatre)

    E momasa ra þeron

    I was with my mom

    An nnmasa ra rona a rakomad

    She will watch a movie with her girlfriend

    Au þansa ra nnya?

    Are you with him?

    Hya galasa ra þeron

    I wasnt with my dog

    Since there is no verb in these sentences, the negation particle “ha” is placed after the pronoun. When “ha” follows the pronoun I, it turns into “hya”. Here is one more complex sentence just for fun, can you understand some of it?

    E þansa ra þand mamonan lubaron semerayen yana sa hakrokt dayana ra

    I was with him at his house, and because I had alot of pent up energy, I couldnt stay in one place without giving in to that guilty pleasure

    Now the last and final type of “with” is “sana”. “Sana” can only be used with one non-living noun, and one living-noun. So, using “sana”, you can create sentences like "The dog with the toy" or "The woman with the watch". Now, unlike the other types of “with” in Ceron which are directly attached onto the noun which the subject is “with”'. “Sana” always gets attached to the living-noun, not the non-living noun

    Tenyasana mona

    The person with the hat

    NOT

    Tenya monasana

    The person with the hat

    Now, here are some more examples

    Tenyasana sap mamon

    The person with the big house

    Luya þand asecsana

    The flower with his lover

    þansana brch a

    He is with his paintings

    Galasana bya a gana sa a buden

    The dog is talking with its quiet toy

    This concludes all you need to know about 'with' in Ceron! Now for a short lesson on 'using' which usally translates to 'with' in English but not in Ceron.

    Expressing “Using” with Nouns

    So in English we can say things like "This table is made with wood" or "I used soap to wash my hands/I washed my hands with soap". See how they include 'with' in the sentence. In Ceron, we use a different word when we want to express something was 'made' with something or when we use something to do something. Lets look at these English sentences first:

    Im washing my hands with soap

    This house is built with wood

    In both these sentences, where 'with' appears, in Ceron, we can exchange them with 'r un' (with vowels) and 'ur un' with consonants, not 'a ca' or any of the other 'with' variants. Here are some examples:

    Lared maremon raenur un a ranmare= This house is built with wood/This house is/was made using wood

    Mene son sopur un a honancen= I wash my hands with soap/I use soap to wash my hands

    See how 'with' is used in English, the word for 'using' is there in Ceron. Here are some more examples so you can familiarize yourself:

    Lared resem raenur un gardan= This table is made with wood

    E sacon a sa reror un sea= I came to school by bike

    Than grogur un edase= He thinks with his brain

    Au padaerur un edase= You think using your feet (idiom, kind of like 'you dont think before you speak')

    E haberur un a gonrun= I study using books
