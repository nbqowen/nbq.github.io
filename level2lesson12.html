Well, Not Well, More, Less, And etc

Well and Not Well

Oftentimes in English, we can say things like "I speak German well" or "How well can you drive?" among other things. In Ceron, we can also say things like that even though there is no real word 'well' 'nice' or 'good'. We create sentences like that using two different words:

a na= Well, nice

a rana= Well, nice

Lets talk about 'a na' first. This word is used to describe P verbs that you do well or in a good manner, for example:

e a boud a na= I drink well

e durden a burden a na= I speak German well

It's very simple to use and is always attached to the end of a sentence. (Even after verbs). The next word, 'a rana' is used to describe NP verbs that you do well or in a good manner. You might also see some words that have 'rana' built into them to describe a word that is nice:

e comadhe a rana= I see well

e aesa a rana= I love well

ranaboud= Trachea or Esophagus

ranaburden= A good speaker

Now unlike in English where we can just attach 'not' before 'well' to create 'not well' we cant do this in Ceron. In Ceron, there is a seperate unrelated word which means to not do something well. That word is:

modara= Not Well

We can use this word with both NP and P words:

e durden a burden modara= I dont speak German well

e a bred modara= I cant paint well

than a baerden modara= He doesnt drive well

an edase modara= She doesnt think well

More, Less

Now in Lesson 9 we learned how to express 'more' of something literally. In English, we can attach 'more' infront of adjectives to create what we call the comparative. Ex, strong, more strong (stronger), confusing, more confusing etc. Now like I said in lesson 9, we cant use 'more' in Ceron to create the comparative or superalative, instead, we use these two grammar particles respectively:

gu= More (Comparative)

na= Most (Superalative)

In all honesty, these particles are rarely used. Although these are considered to be the offical particles which you can use to create the comparative and superalative, many adjectives have their own unique comparative and superlative form. See these words for example:

Gusharon= Prettier

Shaderc= Darker

Sareped= Bigger

Draesc= Worse

Gucane= Kinder

auvanmec= More difficult

Notice how so many of these words have no relation in their comparative form and some of them do. (Ex, Prettier and Kinder both use 'gu' for the comparative form.) It's hard to say this but, overtime you learn what 'sounds right' and what doesnt. Generally speaking, adjectives use one of these suffixes to create the comparative form:

Erc

Ed

Sc

Ac

Ec

There is no rule or logical explanation for when to use a specific form. Now the superlative is created quite similarly by using one of these 2 suffixes which are quite regular:

ar

ca

The first suffix 'ar' is used when the word ends with a consonant. The second suffix 'ca' is used when the word ends with a vowel. Note that NP adjectives emit the 'a' connecter to the noun/verb when using the superalative.

Gusharonden sharonar denare a= Redheads are the prettiest people

Caneca= Kindest

Mene edare a au levelar denare a= You are the most important person in my life

Carenacar= Proudest

aoudar= Hotest

Now so far I have just talked about the comparative and superalative in the 'positive form'. Just like in English, we can create words like "less pretty" and "least pretty" using a rather simple method compared to how we create the positive version:

Saradean= Less

Gran= Least

We can use these words just like how we would use "Less" and "Least" in English:

Saradean mahare= Less water

Gran marep maremon thrarre= The smallest house is over there

Saradean denare= Less people

Gran naran= Least amount of deer

Gran harem= Ugliest

Now it does get a little bit confusing... You might be wondering how can "Gran marep" mean 'smallest' when its composed of the word 'Gran' meaning 'Least' and 'Marep' meaning small, wouldnt these mean 'Least small'? AKA 'Big' in English? Well in English this does infact make sense, but in Ceron, we dont think of it that way. Whenever an adjective is the opposite of a positive adjective/negative in meaning. (Ex, large, small, pretty, ugly, fun, not fun, exciting, boring) We use 'Gran' to express the superalative. (Even though it means 'Least'). Here are some examples to make it less confusing:

Gran marep= Smallest

Gran harem= Ugliest

Gran fward= Coldest

Gran level= Most unimportant

This ONLY applys for the superalative, NOT the comparative:

Gran fward= Coldest

Fwardec= Colder

Fward= Cold

Saradean Fward= Less cold

Aoud= Least cold (Hot)

If you really want to express something like 'least cold' you can add 'marep' before the word although this is rarely used.

Marep fwardLeast cold

An easy way to remember that 'Gran' (meaning least) means 'Most' in English is by associating 'Gran' with a word like 'Grand' or 'Grandeur' to remember that it means 'Most'. Also, incase you forgot how to express 'than' in Ceron, its created by attaching 'a sa' to the person or thing you have 'more/less than':

Lared ca a sa fwardec= This is more cold than that

Than a sa an haremerc a= She is more ugly than him

So far we have covered how to express more with adjectives and nouns, but how do we express more with verbs to create sentences like "I want to run more" or "I drink more"? To create sentences like that, we use this word:

aeMore

Earlier, you learned that 'ae' means 'very', now although this is the case sometimes (usally with adjectives), it also means 'more' (usally with verbs):

E an a sa ae a boud= I drink more than her

E ae remcaumen= I want to run more

E a sa than ae a burden= He talks more than me

E ae a spen= I fight more

Eras ae dacde= They say more

Simple right?

Now before I move on, just remember that most of these words can only be paired with certain types of words. (For example, expressing 'more' with nouns is done using 'L/Lu' but expressing 'less' with nouns is made using 'Saradean'. Just remember that just because its one simple word in English 'more' doesnt mean its that simple in Ceron. You have to think about whether the word is a noun, verb, adjective etc.)

And

And is one of the most frequently used words in the English language and can be used in so many different situations. You might be wondering why I havent introduced such an important word yet, and while I do think I should have introduced it a little bit earlier, 'and' in Ceron is far more intricate and confusing compared to its English counterpart. Just like in many languages, we use different types of 'and' depending on the types of words we are connecting. Generally speaking, there are 3 different types of 'and' we can use:

an= And

Now you might see this word's resemblence to that of the English word 'and' and it's true, this word is brought in from English and is used to connect two nouns. Example:

Gala an faou= Dog and cat

Heud an bonera= Grass and plants

Mahare an chas= Water and tea

The next type of 'and' is used to connect two adjectives that are similar in meaning in terms of sterotypes.

ca= And

This 'and' is most often used to create metaphors or idioms and is mostly used by native speakers. The sterotypes in English do not apply to the ones in Ceron, for example:

Shad ca harem= Dark and ugly

Sharon ca cane= Pretty and kind

Sarep ca carenac= Big and proud

These words are often associated with eachother and so therefore 'ca' is used to connect them. (I wouldnt worry about this one too much).

This last and final type of 'and' is used to connect verbs, adjectives or adverbs together that might relate to eachother or might not. This 'and' is attached after both words at the end.

ac= And

There are many more different kinds of 'and' in Ceron but I see no point in introducing them now. That being said though, I do want to introduce one other 'and' which is used to connect two living-nouns:

ra= And

(This one is placed inbetween like the others, not at the end.)

Bea shadac= Quiet and dark

Caumen burdenac= Running and talking

Edase sraeac= Thinking and writing

Marep fareac= Small and far

Than a batare boudac= He is eating and drinking

anna ra denare= The woman and the man

E ra than serran ra sa= Him and I are going to go to the park

E a dun caumen an than e aesa aun ac dacde= I was walking and he said I love you*

E au a sa caumen burdenac comadhea ra= I just saw you running and talking

Often 'a' can be dropped with P verbs in sentences like these where its obvious. This sentence is also valid though:

E au a caumen burdenac comadhea ra= I just saw you running and talking

*In this sentence 'an' is used, this is because 'an' is also used to connect all the other types of connections outside of the ones metioned. So you use 'an' to connect verbs and pronouns, adjectives and living-nouns etc. It serves as a universal connector.
